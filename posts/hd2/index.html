The Hart Dworkin Debate (Part 2)

Nov 11 2020 07:00 PM EST
Philosophy of Law , Legal Positivism , Natural Law Theory

The Question for Today

What is the relationship between law and morality?

Hart’s Positivism

  • Model of Rules
  • Primary and Secondary Rules
  • The Seperability Thesis
  • The Discretion Thesis

Dworkin’s Critiques of Hart

  • Sometimes judges are bound by moral principles in hard cases
  • If that’s the case then the law in in part constituted by moral principles.

Positivist Responses to Dworkin

  1. Exclusive Legal Positivists
  2. Inclusive Legal Positivism

Exclusive Legal Positivism

  • Dworkin’s principles have a social pedigree (like law), or
  • These principles are just not law

Principles With Social Pedigree

  • Maybe Dworkin’s principles have a social pedigree?
  • Its not relaly the moral content, but maybe judicial customs that explain the normative force.
  • But what about new principles? What can explain these?

Extra Legal Principles

  • In Dworkin’s example, the Judges are bound by the principles, but that doesn’t mean that these principles are part of the law.
  • Example: choice of law issues

Inclusive Legal Positivism

  • Laws might depend on moral tests (if the ROR incorporated them), but that’s just a contingent, historical accident.
  • There’s no necessary connection between law and morality

Dworkin’s Second Critique

  • Positivists are unable to account for theoretical disagreements when interpreting the law.

Dworkin’s Two Distinctions

  1. Propositions of Law vs The Grounds of Law

  2. Empirical Disagreements vs Theoretic Disagreements

Propositions of Law

  • Propositions of Law are statements about what the law “allows or prohibits or entitles them to have.” They are eitehr true or false.

  • “It is illegal for people of New York to drive 120mph down 5th Ave.”

Grounds of Law

  • Propositions of law are true “in virtue of” the grounds of law

  • The NY legislature voted for the speeding bill and the governor signed it.

Empirical Disagreement

  • Disagreements about whether the grounds of law have obtained

  • Did the legislature pass the law with the correct majority, were the proper procedures followed?

Theoretic Disagreement

  • Disagreements “about whether statute books and judicial decisions exhaust the pertinent grounds of law”

Dworkin’s Objection

  • Positivists cannot explain theoretic disagreeements in law because for positivists the grounds of law are empirical facts about the “pedigree” of the law, they are a result of consensus in the legal community.

  • In other words, all disagreements are just empirical disagreements

Tennessee Valley Authority v. Hill

  • Conservationists sue to stop the building of a dam, because it would harm the ecosystem of the endangered snail darter (an endangered fish under the Endangered Species Act).

  • The problem was that the dam was mostly constructed.

The Court sided with the conversationists

  • Justice Burger held for the majority that despite the problems with stopping the project, the text Endangered Species Act requires that the project stop despite the obvious problems in doing so.

The Dissent

  • Justice Powell argued that Courts should not construe the statutes in absurd ways - except when inteded by the legislature.

  • Shutting down the project would lead to such an absurd result and therefore should not be done.

Theoretical Disagreement in TVA

  • Everyone agreed what the law was, the question became: were the words of the statute the only legitimate grounds of the law? Or should the justices incorporate some standard of rationality?

  • Example 2: Riggs vs Palmer

What’s Going on Here - Constructive Interpretation

  • Dworkin thinks you need to determine the point of the practice which is most morally justified

  • Then you determine the grounds of law using that information

Dworkin can explain theoretical disagreements through constructive interpretation.

Disagreements about the grounds of law are at their core about the moral value of the law or how well the law fits it’s purpose.

Burger and Powell have this sort of disagreement - about whether the purpose of the law would be frustrated by stopping the dam project.

Where are We Now?

  • Positivists might respond that these disagreements are about repairing the law or what the law should be

  • Its fashionable to argue that there really is no disagreement - b/c Dworkin’s view of rules is too strict
