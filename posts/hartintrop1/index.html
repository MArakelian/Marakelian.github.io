HLA Hart's The Concept of Law: What Everyone Gets Wrong! [Part 1]

Jun 24 2021 07:00 PM EDT
Legal Postivism , Philosophy of Law

This Thursday we’re taking a look at HLA Hart’s seminal work: The Concept of Law. It was Hart’s most complete formulation of his legal postivism (in part because he updated it over the course of his career).

We’ll take a look at the intro to the newest edition, written by legal philosopher Leslie Green, who brilliantly explains the various parts of Hart’s theory that have been misunderstood. I’m splitting the intro into two streams.

Feel free to read through the preliminary reading notes to get a feel for what we’ll be covering!

Hart’s Message

Law is a social construction

  • There can be no “Pure Theory” of law - law is always political and embedded in a social context that connects it to our other insittutions and values.

  • Green’s List of Misunderstandings:

    1. Doesn’t Hart think that the law is a closed logical system of rules?
    2. Doesn’t Hart think that the law is a good thing, a social achievement that cures defects in other forms of social order?
    3. Doesn’t he think that laws are clear and to be applied by courts without regard for moral values?
    4. Doesn’t he think that law and morality are conceptually separate and to be kept apart?
    5. Doesn’t he think that jurisprudence is value free?
    • NO! Hart doesn’t believe any of these things!

Law as a Social Construction

  • Law is a Social Construction: Law is made of institutional facts like orders and rules, and those are made by people thinking and acting

  • This is the opposite of [[Natural Law Theory]] which posits an eternal and objective law. (and [[Dworkin’s Early Critique of Hart]])

    • For Dworkin, the justifications of our norms are also part of the law. i.e. the prohibition of murder, and our best moral arguments in favor of prohibiting murder are both part of the law.
      • Moreover, those justifications are not social constructs for Dworkin (since they reflect objective moral standards)
      • Hart will reject the idea that those non-constructed, objective justifications are part of the law itself.

  • For Hart, anything that is in law must be posited by a person, which is where the name “legal positivism” comes from (according to Green)

    • [[Hans Kelsen]] was not a constructivist like Hart, because the Grundnorm must be presupposed and not posited.
      • For Kelsen the [[Is-Ought Gap]] ensures that we cannot explain the normativity of the law without some presupposition of normativity in the Grundnorm, in virtue of which all lower norms are valid (or normatively binding).
    • Hart disagrees with Kelsen and insists that jurisprudence is a “descriptive sociology”

Laws, Rules, and Conventions

  • Hart rejects the theories of earlier positivists like [[John Austin]] who argues that laws were “general commands made by a sovereign backed by force” in favor of a theory of law as rules.

    • Law is a union of social rules:
      1. Primary Rules: guide behavior by imposing duties or conferring powers on people
      2. Secondary Rules: identify, alter, and enforce primary rules

  • The Rule of Recognition: A secondary rule which identifies the criteria of legal validity by articulating some process by which a law is created.

    • The ROR is (for Hart) typically some set of social facts, (in a nutshell) –> “Whatever the Queen in Parliament is law”

  • The Practice Theory of Rules: rules are a social construction “made of” practice

    • External Aspect: people act in a regular way in conformance with the rule
    • Internal Aspect: a willingness to use the regularity as a standard to guide and appraise behavior (does not require approval).
    • Objections: Hart wants to explain genuinely normative rule following from brute habits or accidental behavior
      1. There are rules that are not social practices (an individual’s rules)
      2. There are accepted social practices that are not rules (common and accepted practice of surrendering your wallet to an armed robber)
      3. Rules can act as a justification for one’s behavior and not merely as a “sign that there is some justification for it”
    • Hart walks back his Practice theory of rules and later insists that conventional rules are practice rules and that these are the rules that form the ultimate basis of law
      • Rules are conventional if the general conformance with the rule is part of the reason that the rule is accepted.

The Reach of Rules

  • Suppose that understanding rules is important for understanding some part of the law. Is an understanding of rules sufficient for understanding all law?

    • Not all systems of primary and secondary rules are legal –> the rules of the NHL for a set of primary and secondary rules, but these are not legal, even though they look the part.
      • To be legal, a system of rules (1) can and (2) must regulate widely on topics including property, agreements, and the use of force.

  • The Minimum Content of the Legal System

    • The ability to and actual regulation of property, agreements, and use of force.
    • All legal systems are oriented toward some form of good, since regulating this content promoted human survival and human survival is morally good.
      • Hart’s system is therefore not some formalism completely divorced from value judgments

  • There are obviously non-rule legal norms(?) such as: definitions, principles (maybe).

  • An understanding of rules is not sufficient for a complete picture of the law (and the law is not a mere formalism devoid of value judgments)
