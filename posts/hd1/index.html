The Hart Dworkin Debate (Part 1)

Oct 28 2020 07:00 PM EDT
Philosophy of Law , Legal Positivism , Natural Law Theory

The Question for Today

What is the relationship between law and morality?

A (Very) Short History of Legal Positivsm

  • Legal positivism is the thesis that the existence and content of law depends on social facts and not on its merits (Green and Adams, 2019).

Austinian Legal Positivism

  • John Austin (1790-1859) argued that laws are the commands of a sovereign backed by threat of sanctions.

  • This is the commmand theory of law. Notice that it defines the laws entirely through social facts

Hart’s Critique of Austin

  • What about all the law that the command theory leaves out?

    • Contract, property, delegated authority

  • Austin cannot explain why people follow the law (the normative foce of the law).

    • Austin cannot distinguish between the armed burglar and the government

Hart’s Positivism

  1. The Model of Rules

  2. Primary and Secondary Rules

  3. The Separability Thesis

  4. The Discretion Thesis

The Model of Rules

  • Laws are rules, not dictates.

  • Rules are not laws because of some sanction, but because they were created by someone with the proper authority.

Who Gave You The Right?

  • Rule-making authority is itself a product of rules, which means we have two kinds of rules:

  1. Secondary rules that regulate the creation of rules

  2. Primary rules that are the product of secondary rules

The Rule of Recognition

  • The ROR sets forth the conditions that must be satisfied in order for norm to constitute part of the community’s law.

  • It allows for the delegation of power.

  • The ROR is really a constellation of norms, not just one rule.

The Open Texture of Law

  • Rules are inherently ambiguous

  • For any rule, there will necessarily be some scenario where the “law runs out”

  • No Vehicles in the Park

The Discretion Thesis

  • In hard cases, judges are forced to go beyond the law to apply non-legal principles to adjudicate cases.

The Seperability Thesis

  • What makes a norm law depends on its satisfying the conditions set forth in the relevant rule of recognition, not some moral standard.

Putting Hart’s Theory Together

  1. The Model of Rules

  2. The Rule of Recognition

  3. The Separability Thesis

  4. The Discretion Thesis

Dworkin’s Critique

Dworkin makes two critiques in The Model of Rules I

  1. The Objection to Discretion

  2. The Objection to Seperability

1) Judges and Principles

  • There are some cases in which judges view themselves as being bound legally by principles that they use to decide hard cases (Discretion Thesis)

  • These principles are not rules, they are not normative because of their pedigree (the social facts), but because of their moral content.

Henningsen

  • Henningsen buys a car from a dealership.

  • The Dealership has him sign a contract which disclaims their liability for anything but defective parts. It uses very small print and was not brought to Mr. Henningsen’s attention (he admitted he didn’t read it)

Henningsen (Cont')

  • After, the a manufacturing defect in the car causes
    a dangerous crash.

  • The court held that the defendant dealership was liable for the cost to replace the car, in part because they should not benefit from selling defective/dangerous cars.

  • These were principles, not legal rules. The Justices thought themselves bound not because of the pedigree of the principles but because of their moral content.

2) The Law and Morality

  • If these principles are binding (and part of the law) they bind sometimes in virtue of their moral content and not because of their pedigree according to social facts.
