Natural Law Theories

Aug 21 2020 07:00 PM EDT
Philosophy of Law , Natural Law Theory

The Two Main Questions

Q: What would a full understanding of the law include?

Q: Would a full understanding include some standard of rationality or only include some set of social facts?

The Two Main Principles of Law

  1. Law is necessarily a socially recognized standard of conduct

  2. Law is necessarily a rational standard of conduct.

Reasons for Action

  • The motivation for thinking (2) is that we need to explain how the law can provide us with reasons for us to act

What is Natural Law Anyway?

  • Dworkin: natural lawyers argue that

what the law is depends in some way on what the law should be

The Two Main Principles of Law

  1. Law is necessarily a socially recognized standard of conduct

  2. Law is necessarily a rational standard of conduct.

Why Does this Matter?

  • MLK Jr. (Letter From Birmingham a Jail)

I would be the first to advocate obeying just laws. One has not only a legal but a moral responsibility to obey just laws. Conversely, one has a moral responsibility to disobey unjust laws. I would agree with St. Augustine that “an unjust law is no law at all.”

Morals vs Rationality

  • Most Natural Law theorists today argue that the law relfects some rational standard, not necessarily a moral one

  • Example: Paying Taxes?

Strong vs Weak Natural Law

  • The strong natural law view interprets the natural law thesis as stating an existence condition for law.

  • The weak natural law view, by contrast, interprets the claim as stating a non-defectiveness condition for law.

Three Types of Argument

  1. Hermeneutic

  2. Functionalist

  3. Speech Act

Hermeneutic Arguments (Finnis)

  • Hermeneutic arguments seek to understand and justify the normative force of our social practices.

  • That is, how to understand the normative force of the law. How it provides us with reasons for action.

  • Draws on arguments from Aquinas.

What is the practical point of law?

  • For the law to provide us with reasons for action, it must be rational for us to follow the law
  • So the law can’t just prevent us from hurting each other, it has to give us positive reason to comply

Social and Normative Facts

  • Finnis argues that the law should be understood in term of both social facts, and as directed at the common good of the community
    • This way, it can engage with the rationality of the people in the community and provide them with reasons for action.

The Common Good

  • A law that is not directed at the promotion of the common good does not engage in the rational agency of the community and therefore is defective
    • It may be some sort of law in a weak sense, a defective law (weak natural law theory)

Functionalist Arguments (Moore, Fuller, Murphy)

  • The law is understood functionally, as a system to direct human action through some particular method or for some specific purpose.
  • Anything that fails in this purpose is either defective, or no law at all.

Directing Action

  • These arguments all claim that the function of law is to direct action.
  • If this is the purpose of law, it needs to provide reasons for one to take the action in question. Otherwise, it will be in some sense defective.

Speech Act Arguments (Alexy, Murphy)

  • These arguments claim that law is a form of Speech Act.
  • A Speech Act is some form of speech which is performed for the purpose of creating some effect in the listener apart from the expression of the proposition.

Moral Correctness

  • Alexy argues that all legal systems claim moral correctness
  • Part of the speech act here is to create a legitimizing effect, if the law is immoral, then this effect is not produced and the speech act/law is defective.

The Law as a Demand

  • Murphy argues that the law is in part a demand on those in the legal jurisdiction
  • If the law demands that you do some action x then it must provide you with reasons to x, otherwise it is defective.

Review

  • A complete description of the law is (1) social facts, and (2) some rational standard of conduct
  • NLT seek to explain the law’s normative force
    • Hermeneutic
    • Functionalist
    • Speech Acts
  • These arguments rely generally function of law to direct peoples' actions

Complications

  1. What is rationality anyway?

  2. Are we sure that the law has normative force?
